# Product and editorial decisions

## The old centre of gravity

The earlier product placed the faculty catalogue, programme form and upload interaction at roughly the same conceptual level. It was technically capable, but the interface still asked the student to understand the system before the system had explained what kind of judgment it could produce.

## The new centre of gravity

The redesign organises the product around a student’s decision problem:

### Where am I now?

- credits recognised within the selected route;
- senior-level credit;
- represented requirements;
- major progress;
- represented readmission and distinction indicators.

### What is blocking me?

- incomplete blocking rules;
- repeated failures;
- prerequisite barriers;
- missing evidence;
- provisional source authority;
- explicit institutional-verification questions.

### What can I do next?

- courses visible within the route;
- represented prerequisite eligibility;
- why each option appears;
- limitations attached to the suggestion.

The phrase **route-visible** is preferred to **registerable**. Registration depends on operational and discretionary conditions the current product does not possess.

## Editorial voice

The interface avoids false reassurance and false alarm.

It does not say:

- “You are safe” when only a provisional threshold was assessed;
- “You can register” when timetable and capacity are unknown;
- “You will graduate” when institutional discretion remains;
- “Your transcript is wrong” when the mismatch may be route selection or incomplete parsing.

It says what happened, what evidence was used and what should be confirmed.

## Visual system

The redesign uses:

- dark institutional green for authority without imitating UCT branding;
- warm paper and cream backgrounds to avoid a generic software-dashboard appearance;
- a serif display typeface for academic seriousness;
- a restrained sans-serif for operational clarity;
- gold as an evidence and navigation accent;
- green, amber and red only for conclusion states.

The report is deliberately printable and avoids depending on charts. Requirements are readable as statements, not merely coloured progress bars.

## Administration stance

The data desk is visible but not writable. This is not an unfinished button set. It is an architectural statement:

> user-friendliness for administrators must not mean casual mutability of institutional rules.

The future editor should use structured rule forms, source references, impact previews and review workflows. It should not expose raw JSON as the main authoring experience, and it should not allow departments to redefine the shared ontology.
